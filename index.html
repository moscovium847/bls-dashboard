What is Business Law and Society (BLS)?

BLS articles are those that address how business entities interact with, affect, or are shaped by broader societal interests, values, or institutions — particularly where the focus goes beyond firm value maximization or internal governance mechanics. This includes scholarship on corporate social responsibility, environmental and climate obligations, stakeholder welfare, DEI, corporate purpose debates, and social enterprise. Articles focused on traditional corporate law doctrine, governance mechanics, or financial regulation are classified as non-BLS.

BLS Tone Analysis: Positive vs. Negative

Among the 1,407 BLS-coded articles, each was classified as taking a positive or negative normative stance toward the relationship between business law and society. Positive articles argue that corporations can or should address social concerns. Negative articles critique corporate conduct on BLS issues, or raise concerns about the scholarly or corporate direction toward an increased focus on BLS.

Methodology

Stage 1 — Data gathering and cleaning

I worked with law librarians to develop and refine Boolean searches in LexisNexis and Westlaw designed to identify corporate law and corporate governance articles. The initial searches generated more than 7,000 results across the two databases. A research assistant manually downloaded those results. We then cleaned the dataset by removing duplicate articles, articles published in non-U.S. journals, articles published in professional or practitioner-oriented journals rather than scholarly law journals, articles outside the scope of corporate law or corporate governance, and student-written notes and comments. After these exclusions, the final corpus contained 3,266 unique articles.

Stage 2 — Manual coding

My research assistant and I manually coded a random subset of 874 articles. We first classified each article into one or two of thirty-five predefined corporate law and governance topic areas. We then coded each article for whether its substantive focus fell within the BLS framework. Articles were coded as "0" if they did not fit the BLS definition and "1" if they did. For articles coded as BLS articles, we also coded the author's treatment of the BLS issue — specifically, whether the article was supportive of or critical of a business-society approach.

Stage 3 — AI-assisted coding

We used Harvey AI to apply the coding protocol to the full corpus. Harvey allowed us to apply a uniform coding workflow to a large set of documents. We uploaded the articles and ran a workflow that generated structured responses for each document, including the article's corporate law topic, BLS classification, treatment of the BLS issue, author, year, and journal. Harvey returned the results in tabular form, which allowed us to sort, filter, spot-check, and export the data for analysis.

To validate the AI-assisted coding, we compared Harvey's classifications against the manually coded subset. The manual coding served as a benchmark for evaluating whether Harvey applied the coding protocol consistently. We then revised the prompts and coding instructions to improve consistency between the manual and AI-assisted coding. The final analysis therefore combines close manual review with AI-assisted coding at scale.

Limitations

All BLS and topic classifications are based on the researcher's judgment. The dual-tag limit means articles spanning three or more themes may be simplified. Boolean search terms may not capture all relevant scholarship, and coverage depends on the completeness of Westlaw and LexisNexis databases.
